Step 2

My analysis of Nielsen's heuristics was based on search results for the topic "presidential election".

  1. Visibility of the system - When the user applies specific filters, they can be easily eliminated by clicking the "(x)" to the right of the filter at the top of the search results. Also, you can press the button "back to results" to easily maneuver back to the results page instead of using the back button. This creates easy navigation and bread crumbing for the user.
  2. Match between the system and the real world - The collection filters next to the search bar include, all collections, archives and special collections, Oliviera Lima library, and research repository. It is not clear to the average researcher the difference or context around Oliver Lima Library and research repository. It would be helpful for the website to have a navigation explanation for these on an FAQ page.
  3. User control and freedom - Searches within documents and in the search bar take a while to load. Sometimes they never fully load and you need to refresh or hit the back button. There is also no cancel button which can leave you stuck for a few minutes. This can lead to a frustrated user who's impatience might result in using another site.
  4. Consistency and Standards - The search bars and key information is easy to find and meets industry standards. It was easy to navigate for the user.
  5. Error prevention - Top search results often didn't seem relevant to the search until you read the document. The words searched often weren't in the title or anywhere in the list of results. This made the search results confusing on relevancy until you searched within the documents themselves. It would be helpful for key words to be displayed with the search results.
  6. Recognition rather than recall - The search bar changes locations when moving from a general search to within a document search. Additionally, there is a large magnifying glass that restarts your search instead of searching within the results. It's purpose is unclear and confusing to the user.
  7. Flexibility and efficiency of use - After clicking on a search result, you can see the trail of how you got there and can go back to the beginning or back to other steps.
  8. Aesthetic and minimalist design - Sometimes a side tab pops up with similar search results that is distracting and not relevant. Overall the website is free from adds and confusing or distracting images.
  9. Help users - When searching for items in the text it will not notify the user if no results are found. This makes the user uncertain if the page just didn't load properly or if there are no results.
  10. Help and documentation - There is no help button, chat bot, or FAQ page. However, there is a thorough contact us page with multiple phone numbers and emails to reach out to.

Step 3

Overall, the Tower is easy to navigate and has many helpful filter features. They are clearly invested in helping researchers find good materials based on the robust contact us page and the links to breadcrumbs. Where the Tower falls short is in loading times and identifying helpful resources. Sometimes a source will not load and a user has to refresh or reload the page multiple times. This was frustrating as a user and time consuming. Lastly, the search results were often based upon contents of a document and not the title or subject line. This meant that search results were not obviously applicable since there was no key word identifier. Please see below my examples of these successes and pitfalls.
